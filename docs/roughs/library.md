(Notably, you CANNOT mix propositions and lambda expressions; the lambdas are untyped terms of the logic, playing the same role in this logic that natural numbers do in peano arithmetic).

Starting with the most general and expressing things, as much as possible, as special cases of more general things. What should this look like?
> I would ideally like to build up enough mathemactics to formalize mathematical physics (in e.g. the vein of Urs Shreiber), and I'd like to actually leverage formulations that are well suited for this setting (e.g. impredicative encodings and the relation of such to universal properties).
> The logic is a logic of lambda calculus. Some kind of streamlining of all of mathematics with broader lambda calculus theory (type systems?) may be appropriate.

This setting has a name-shaped hole in the literature that's worth knowing about first, because it tells you what the unifying description is: what you have is essentially an untyped variant of Krivine's AF2 / second-order functional arithmetic, and its semantics is a realizability tripos over your λ-theory. That fact should be the organizing principle of the whole library. You are not "formalizing mathematics in a logic that happens to have lambdas"; you are developing, syntactically, the internal mathematics of the realizability topos generated by the untyped λ-calculus. Nearly every design decision below is a corollary of taking that seriously.

Here is the skeleton I'd propose, from the bottom, with the reason each layer is *specifically* well-expressed in your setting.

**Layer 0 — λ-theory as the "arithmetic core."** Combinators, pairing, Church/Scott encodings, and crucially the fixed-point theorem (Y). Unlike PA's ℕ, your ground sort is a reflexive object: every term-definable endofunction has a fixed point. This is both your superpower (general recursion, self-interpretation) and your discipline problem (no term-level totality). Observable: prove `∀f. ∃x. f x = x` immediately; it will shape everything, because it means "totality" and "termination" must live at the predicate level, never the term level.

**Layer 1 — the μν-calculus over the predicate lattice.** Second-order quantification makes the lattice of predicates a complete Heyting algebra with impredicative meets, so Knaster–Tarski is directly definable: `μΦ(x) := ∀P. (∀y. Φ(P)(y) → P(y)) → P(x)`, dually ν via ∃-encoding. This single construction is your inductive/coinductive engine, and it subsumes what type theories provide as primitive inductive families. Frege-style definitions like `N(x) := ∀P. P 0 → (∀y. P y → P (s y)) → P x` are exactly `μ` instances, and induction is definitionally free. Build this layer once, abstractly, with monotone predicate transformers as the interface; every datatype, every accessibility predicate, every formal-topology cover relation later is an instantiation. Also put here: Leibniz equality, and modal operators on predicates — ¬¬, closure operators, nuclei. Nuclei will later give you subtoposes and locale quotients, and if your logic is intuitionistic (it should be, given realizability semantics), ¬¬ is how you recover classical fragments locally rather than globally.

**Layer 2 — PERs as the notion of "set," relations as the notion of "family."** This is the load-bearing decision. βη-equality is the wrong equality for almost all mathematics (ℚ, ℝ, quotient groups, unordered structures), so the base notion of collection should be a partial equivalence relation on terms from day one, with plain predicates as the degenerate case. Two facts make this elegant rather than bureaucratic in your setting specifically: (a) quotients are free — a quotient of a PER is just a coarser PER, no setoid-hell, no quotient axioms; (b) *dependent families need no new machinery* — a family of PERs over a PER A is literally just a ternary relation R(a, x, y), because everything is untyped. This is the standard realizability treatment of dependency, and here it's native. From this layer you can present the whole thing as a category-with-families / tripos structure: your "type-theoretic macros" (Π, Σ, identity, W) should be defined once as operations on (indexed) PERs, and then all downstream mathematics is written against that interface. Also define retracts (`r ∘ r = r`) and note the Karoubi envelope of the βη-monoid sits inside PERs as the "representable" fragment — Scott's data-types-as-retracts becomes a theorem about a subcategory, not a competing foundation.

**Layer 3 — parametricity as an axiom schema.** Impredicative encodings in raw second-order logic give you *weak* initiality: existence of the mediating map, not uniqueness. You have two honest options: refine every encoding with its induction predicate (the Awodey–Frey–Speight move, which works but must be redone per encoding), or adopt a relational parametricity schema once. I'd strongly recommend the latter, because your logic is almost exactly Plotkin–Abadi logic minus types, and in that logic parametricity is precisely what upgrades encodings to genuine universal properties — initial algebras, final coalgebras, and limits all get their uniqueness clauses as free theorems (Bainbridge–Freyd–Scedrov–Scott is the reference for why this works; Wadler's Girard–Reynolds papers for the logical mechanics). Relational lifting is second-order definable in your system, so the schema is stateable internally. You'd want to verify it against your intended semantics, but relational PER models are the standard witness of consistency. Observable checkpoint: after adopting the schema, prove that the Church-encoded ℕ satisfies the *full* universal property of an NNO (uniqueness of the recursor up to PER-equality), and that `∀X. X → X` is a singleton. If those go through, the whole "definitions are universal properties, constructions are encodings" methodology is unlocked.

**Layer 4 — category theory with an explicit size discipline.** Small category = term-coded category (a term packaging carriers and composition, with hom-PERs); large category = predicate-level structure that exists only schematically, via your macro facility. The category of PERs itself is large, so it is a scheme, not an object — but here is the payoff of impredicativity: limits of *term-coded* diagrams of PERs are directly definable by ∀-encodings, which is the proof-theoretic shadow of Hyland's theorem that PERs form a small complete category inside the effective topos. Complete small categories are classically impossible; your setting is one of the few places they (in shadow form) exist, and this is exactly why impredicative encodings of universal constructions work at all. Organize the layer so that adjunction is the primitive concept, limits/colimits/monads are derived, and free constructions are the flagship application — free monoids, free groups, free anything are what ∀-encodings do best (`Free(X) := ∀A. Structure(A) → (X → A) → A`, refined per Layer 3). Lawvere theories deserve first-class status here: a Lawvere theory is a *small* (term-coded) category, models are product-preserving functors into PERs, i.e., just relations — so all of universal algebra becomes an instance of functor categories without any size anxiety.

**Layer 5 — numbers and point-free topology.** ℕ from Layer 3; ℤ, ℚ as quotient PERs (free, per Layer 2); ℝ as a PER on Cauchy-sequence codes. For topology, resist point-set: frames are large, but *presentations* of frames are small, so formal topology (Sambin) — a cover relation on a term-coded base, defined as a μ from Layer 1 — is the natively well-expressed option. Also look hard at Taylor's Abstract Stone Duality: it is a λ-calculus-based axiomatic topology and is philosophically the closest existing thing to "topology done in a logic of λ-terms." A decision you must make explicitly here: whether to adopt synthetic computability axioms (Church's thesis / KLS-style continuity). Realizability validates them, they make "every function ℝ→ℝ is continuous" a theorem, and they give the library a distinctive character — Bauer's synthetic computability is essentially the mathematics your model wants to do. But note the tension with physics below.

**Layer 6 — the physics-facing layer: axiomatize, don't construct.** Schreiber's program is differential cohesion: a string of adjoint idempotent (co)monads (ʃ ⊣ ♭ ⊣ ♯, plus the infinitesimal refinement ℜ ⊣ ℑ ⊣ &) on a topos of spaces. The crucial observation for you: modalities are predicate transformers with equations, which is *exactly* what second-order logic expresses well — you already built the machinery in Layer 1. So the right move is an axiomatic interface: a "cohesive structure" is a second-order predicate on a tuple of modality-codes, and physics is developed against that interface. Do not try to construct smooth sets from realizability directly: there is no known realizability model of full SDG (Kock–Lawvere lives in sheaf toposes over smooth algebras), and realizability's native character is computability, not smoothness. The reconciliation is the interface pattern — assume a ring object satisfying Kock–Lawvere axiomatically where needed, exactly as one assumes a group when doing group theory. Separately, sheaves on a *small* site are constructible: a presheaf of PERs on a term-coded site is just an indexed relation, so smooth-sets-as-sheaves-on-CartSp is available as a *model* of the interface once ℝ exists, at least 1-truncated. The honest caveat, which you should design around rather than ignore: your logic has no native ∞-groupoids, so Schreiber's higher gauge theory is out of direct reach; the realistic targets are the 0- and 1-truncated levels (gauge fields as cocycles valued in groupoids-as-term-coded-categories), with internal complete Segal objects as the speculative route upward.

**Layer 7 — reflection as a first-class library citizen.** Mogensen–Scott self-encoding gives you quote/eval for your own ground sort, which typed assistants have to sweat for. A verified self-interpreter plus soundness lemmas for decision procedures (commutative monoid normalization, ring normalization, etc.) makes proof-by-reflection the default automation strategy, and NbE is the natural implementation. This layer should be built early — right after Layer 3 — because reflection amortizes across everything above it.

Three cross-cutting disciplines to fix in writing before line one: (1) the **three-equality problem** — every definition must declare whether it respects βη, its ambient PER, or only logical equivalence, and your macro layer should track this the way typed systems track types, because it is the single most common source of rot in setoid-style libraries; (2) **interface-first definitions** — every concept defined once, at the most general level at which it is still term-expressible, as a second-order predicate on coded structures, with instances as theorems; (3) **the small/large boundary** is a documented convention enforced by macros, never improvised.

Threads to pull, roughly ordered by how much they'd constrain the design: (a) the exact parametricity schema — Plotkin–Abadi-style binary relational lifting for your untyped setting, and what your semantics validates; (b) the CwF-of-PERs macro layer in detail, i.e., precisely which dependent type theory your logic hosts (I'd argue: extensional MLTT with an impredicative universe of propositions, quotients, and general recursion at the term level); (c) whether to commit to synthetic computability axioms globally or scope them as a modality/subtopos via a nucleus from Layer 1; (d) how far the 1-truncated cohesion story actually gets toward, say, classical Chern–Simons or electromagnetism-as-cocycles; (e) internal universes via truth-predicate-style μ-definitions (Aczel's Frege structures are the relevant prior art) — whether a universe of codes-for-PERs with strong closure is definable in your logic or needs an axiom. Also worth stating: is the logic intuitionistic or classical, and does it have first-order ∃ over terms with a choice principle? Both answers materially change Layers 2 and 5.

> The logic is classical, but it does NOT have a choice principle, in the sense that, if you eliminate a ∃ you have to explicitly declare which term you are using as a witness.

Classical-but-choiceless changes the picture more than it first appears, and mostly in ways that sharpen the architecture rather than damage it. Let me go through the consequences in order of how much they restructure the previous plan.

**The semantic home moves from intuitionistic realizability to Krivine-land.** Classical second-order logic over λ-terms is, up to presentation, the setting of Krivine's classical realizability, and the tripos-theoretic packaging is Streicher's "Krivine realizability as a tripos" and Miquel's implicative algebras — which you already know from your proof-assistant work, and which are the right invariant here precisely because they unify forcing and realizability under a classical regime. One relevant import from that literature: Krivine's models deliberately *refute* choice principles while often preserving dependent choice via extra machinery (quote, bar recursion); your system's refusal of choice is therefore not a defect to route around but the native condition of the intended semantics. Storage operators are the other import worth knowing: they are the mechanism by which classical proofs of ∃-statements over *data predicates* (your μ-defined N, lists, etc.) still yield computable witnesses, which matters for your reflection layer.

**The tracked/logical gap becomes the library's central axis.** Without choice — not even unique choice, given your witness rule — there are two inequivalent notions of function between PERs: a *tracked map* (a λ-term respecting the PERs) and a *functional relation* (a formula proven total and single-valued). Classically you can prove existence and uniqueness of outputs all day without producing a term. With choice these collapse; in your system the gap is real, load-bearing, and — this is the reorganization — should be measured rather than hidden. Concretely: the library gets a two-track structure. The *truth track* is ordinary classical mathematics at the proposition level (where you have roughly the strength of full second-order arithmetic, assuming you have unrestricted comprehension — confirm this, it matters). The *realization track* asks which functional relations are term-tracked. The gap between the tracks is exactly what the Weihrauch-degree literature classifies, and I'd steal its organization wholesale: a classically-true ∀∃ statement gets filed with its realizability status — tracked, tracked-relative-to-an-oracle-parameter, or provably untracked. Observable, and a good first exercise: real-number comparison. Prove classically that for Cauchy reals x < y ∨ x ≥ y; then prove there is no term deciding it (reduce a halting-style predicate to it through a term-coded sequence); then find the weakest side information (e.g., a witness that x ≠ y) making it tracked. That triple — classical truth, non-realizability, repair — is the pattern hundreds of library entries will follow.

A discipline follows directly from your ∃-elimination rule: **export lemmas in Skolemized, witnessed form.** A statement ∀x ∃y R(x,y) sitting in the library is nearly useless to downstream proofs, since eliminating it demands a witness the consumer doesn't have. The exportable form is a pair: a term t and a proof of ∀x. R(x, t x) — or, when no such t exists, the explicitly relational statement flagged as truth-track. This also settles how structures are represented: a group is a term tuple (carrier-PER code, operation, inverse, unit) together with a property predicate, never "a PER such that there exist operations." Your macro layer should make the witnessed form the default shape of every definition.

**The parametricity layer largely dissolves — quantify over relations instead of assuming a schema.** This is the pleasant surprise. In the typed setting, ∀X. X → X is a type whose inhabitants are bare terms, and parametricity is an *axiom* constraining them. In your setting, "t inhabits ∀X. X → X" is a *defined predicate*, and if you define it by quantifying over all binary relations (or all PERs) rather than all unary predicates — Poly(t) := ∀R. ∀x x′. R(x,x′) → R(t x, t x′) — then the classical relations you quantify over include every singleton and every graph, and the free theorems fall out by instantiation. Instantiate R at {(a,a)} and Poly(t) forces t a =βη a: polymorphic terms are the identity, as a theorem. Likewise uniqueness of the mediating map out of a relationally-encoded initial algebra is provable by instantiating at the graph of the competing map. Classicality helps here — more relations available means tighter intersections — and the only cost is an adequacy obligation per encoding: show the relational encoding is inhabited by the intended terms (for N, instantiate at the metatheoretically-defined "is βη-equal to a numeral" predicate, which your comprehension supplies). So Layer 3 becomes a *definitional style* — relational encodings as the default, predicate encodings derived by diagonal instantiation — rather than an axiom schema needing a model check. Exercises: (1) prove any t inhabiting the relational ∀X. X → X → X is βη-equal to one of the two projections; (2) define the relational encoding of the initial algebra of a term-coded polynomial functor and prove *both* existence and uniqueness of folds; (3) find an encoding where the relational and predicate versions provably differ on open terms, to see why the distinction is not pedantry.

**Analysis: everything is a modulus, and Dedekind reals live upstairs.** Two choice-sensitive facts. First, without countable choice, Cauchy completeness of Cauchy reals fails *for logical sequences* — but survives for term-coded sequences carrying explicit moduli, because the limit construction is then a term. So the discipline is: a sequence is a term, always, and convergence data is always a modulus term. This makes your Layer 5 internal Weihrauch/TTE-style computable analysis with a classical metatheory — representations are your PERs, computable maps are your tracked maps, and the classical truth track supplies the ordinary theorems of analysis in choice-free form. Second, a Dedekind cut is a predicate on ℚ-codes, and predicates are not terms in your system, so Dedekind reals are not a PER — they are a *large* (schematic, predicate-level) collection. That is a genuine structural fact: your term-world has no power objects, and the classical topos it presents lives at the functional-relation level. Cauchy reals are the set-like reals; Dedekind reals are truth-track apparatus.

**Algebra: Zorn is gone, and everything is hereditarily countable anyway.** Every PER is a subquotient of the term model, so every set-like structure is externally countable, and the classical-but-choiceless pathologies (fields without algebraic closures, vector spaces without bases) get resolved the computable-algebra way: work with explicitly enumerated structures and build closures, maximal objects, and bases by explicit stage-wise term constructions. Note internal countability is *not* free — a tracked surjection ℕ → A can fail to exist (the PER of total term-functions ℕ → ℕ is the standard example) — so "enumerated structure" is extra data, carried as a term, and it's worth an exercise to prove internally that ℕ → ℕ-total-functions admits no tracked enumeration by internalizing the diagonal argument.

**The physics layer gains a firewall and loses a naïve option.** The Kock–Lawvere axiom is inconsistent with LEM *when stated for arbitrary functions*, so the previous plan's "assume a KL ring axiomatically" is dead as written. But the tracked/logical gap is exactly the firewall that might rescue it: the classical refutation of KL manufactures a discontinuous function by case-split, which in your system produces only a functional relation, not a tracked map. So synthetic axioms — KL, Brouwerian continuity, anything of that family — must be scoped to tracked maps only, where they no longer collide with propositional LEM. Whether a classical realizability model actually validates tracked-KL is, to my knowledge, open in your exact configuration and needs a model check before you build on it; I would not assert it. The safer and cleaner route for Schreiber-style content: since sheaves on a term-coded site are indexed PERs and the internal logic of an internal (pre)sheaf category is intuitionistic *regardless of the ambient logic being classical*, do the differential-geometric internal reasoning one level up, inside an internal topos where LEM legitimately fails, keeping the ambient classical logic as the metatheory. The cohesion modalities are unaffected by all of this — they were predicate transformers with equations before and still are.

Threads, deepest-first as usual: (a) work through the relational-encoding calculus properly — which universal properties are theorems under relation-quantification, where dinaturality is needed, and the precise adequacy obligations, since this replaces an axiom schema with a proof style and deserves rigor; (b) design the two-track bookkeeping concretely — what the macro layer records per lemma (tracked witness, oracle-relative witness, Weihrauch class, truth-only) and how proofs compose across tracks; (c) chase the tracked-KL consistency question through the Krivine/Miquel model literature, since it decides whether physics is done via scoped synthetic axioms or exclusively inside internal sheaf toposes; (d) the storage-operator story for witness extraction from classical proofs over data predicates, which determines how much computational content your reflection layer can recover from truth-track proofs. Two facts I need from you before (a) or (b) can be made fully precise: whether second-order comprehension is unrestricted (full impredicative) or stratified somehow, and whether ∃ is a primitive with your witness rule or defined from ∀ and ¬ — the witness rule suggests primitive, and the two choices give genuinely different Skolemization disciplines.

**The continuum as a terminal coalgebra, natively.** The right universal property is known: Freyd's "Algebraic Real Analysis" characterizes the closed interval as the final coalgebra of the ordered-wedge functor X ↦ X ∨ X on bipointed objects (glue two copies of X end-to-end; the interval is the unique fixed point with its two halves), and Pavlović–Pratt's "The continuum as a final coalgebra" does the analogous job for the irrationals/full line via the continued-fraction functor X ↦ ℕ × X. The concrete presentation adapted to your setting is the signed-digit one: a real in [−1,1] is a term t that is a productive stream over digits {−1, 0, 1}, i.e. t satisfies SD := ν(P ↦ λt. Digit(hd t) ∧ P(tl t)). Now the reasons this is *natively* well-expressed, point by point. First, ν costs you nothing: νΦ(x) := ∃P. (∀y. P y → Φ(P)(y)) ∧ P(x) is the dual Knaster–Tarski construction, and the coinduction principle — every Φ-invariant is contained in νΦ — is definitionally immediate, no axiom, exactly as induction was for μ. (Classically you could also define ν as ¬μ¬, but keep the ∃-form primary: it interacts correctly with your witness rule, as below.) Second, corecursion *terms* are free: unfold f s := Y (λu.λs. pair (out₁ (f s)) (u (out₂ (f s)))) s exists because your ground sort has general fixed points — the thing typed assistants need guardedness checkers and primitive corecursion for is a two-line definition here, with productivity discharged separately as a ν-proof. This is the coinductive mirror of the point from before that totality lives at the predicate level: Y hands you every corecursive definition; SD-membership is where the obligation lands. Third, the *equality* of the PER is itself coinductive, which is where the flavor you're asking for actually resides: define x ≤ y as a ν-relation (the largest relation closed under the digit-comparison step) and x < y as a μ-relation (finitary evidence: some finite prefix already separates them), then ≈ := ≤ ∧ ≥. This polarity assignment — strict order inductive, non-strict order coinductive, equality coinductive — is the standard one from Berger's and Ciaffaglione–Di Gianantonio's certified exact-real developments, and it slots directly into your two-track bookkeeping: trichotomy is a truth-track theorem (classical logic at the predicate level), while the tracked comparisons are exactly the μ-side ones, and the non-tracked status of ≤-decision is the first entry in the Weihrauch ledger from last time. Fourth, the hierarchy inverts, which is your top-down mandate realized: dyadic rationals are the *eventually-constant* streams and ℚ the eventually-periodic ones — a μ-condition carved out of the ν-carrier — so rationals are special reals rather than reals being towers over rationals. ℕ enters as observation depth, not substrate. Fifth, the function space is an object, not a macro: Berger's stream-processor representation codes a uniformly continuous function [−1,1] → [−1,1] as a wellfounded-reading, productive-writing tree — an interleaved μ-inside-ν predicate on terms (μ: read finitely many input digits; ν: emit output digits forever). Since trees are terms, tracked ℝ → ℝ is a PER, composition is a term-level algorithm on trees, and the modulus-of-continuity discipline from last time becomes structural rather than bookkept. Had you instead defined the function space as "the tracked maps," you'd be quantifying over trackers inside a predicate — fine — but the tree coding gives you the function space *with* an intrinsic notion of observation, which is what makes it compose with the coalgebraic apparatus.

**Strong dinaturality is the right primitive, and it is exactly your relational-instantiation style.** The relational encodings I proposed — quantify over relations, instantiate at graphs and singletons — are, categorically, the *strong* (Barr/Mulry) dinaturality condition, not plain dinaturality, and the distinction carries the whole weight. Plain dinaturality is the hexagon condition and dinatural transformations do not compose in the general case, and dinaturality and parametricity do not necessarily correspond directly — the type of Church numerals ∀X.(X→X)→(X→X) is inhabited exactly by the naturals, while the collection of dinatural transformations ξ : Xˣ → Xˣ in Set is a proper class. Strong dinaturality replaces the hexagon's mediating morphism with a span/pullback vertex — quantification over *relations* rather than maps — and unlike dinatural transformations, strong dinatural transformations can always be composed, and they have close connections to parametricity. The theorem that makes this your engine rather than a slogan is Ghani–Uustalu–Vene's: a U_F-cone structure with vertex C is the same thing as a strong dinatural transformation from Hom(F−,−) to Hom(C,−), from which they derive that the Church encoding *with* strong dinaturality is genuinely initial — existence and uniqueness of folds — and dually justify the destroy/unfold side for coalgebras; Pavlović and Ghani–Uustalu–Vene justified shortcut deforestation and Church encodings with strong dinaturality. > Can we seat this as a special case of a more general construction allowing us to talk about other (small?) categories, e.g. categories of groups, of other kinds of spaces, etc?

Yes, and the seating is sharper than "the interval is one final coalgebra among many," because your stratification dictates a specific size architecture, and once that's fixed, the general construction is forced. Let me build it in the order the dependencies run.

**The three-tier size architecture, made official.** Tier A: *wire-coded data* — anything given by finite syntax packaged as a term: finite categories, Lawvere theories, finite-limit sketches, sites with μ-defined coverage, finite presentations of algebras, and (the new entry below) containers. Tier B: *definable categories* — categories whose objects are tuples mixing relations and wires (a group is a carrier-relation plus operation-wires) and whose homs are PERs of tracked maps. These are not objects, but they are *quantifiable*: "for all groups G" elaborates to ∀A ∀ops, mixed second- and first-order quantification, which your comprehension supports directly. The presheaf categories from last time, Mod(T) for any Tier-A theory T, categories of coalgebras — all Tier B. Tier C: relation-transformers — functors *between* Tier-B categories, the modality assignments — which remain macro-schemata by the stratification. The design consequence: every general theorem should be pushed down as many tiers as possible, because a Tier-A statement is a single internal theorem quantifying over wires, a Tier-B statement is a single internal theorem with second-order quantifiers, and only Tier C forces schema-hood.

**Containers are the move that makes "for all functors" internal.** The obstruction to a general (co)algebra theory in your system is that an endofunctor on PERs is a predicate transformer, hence a macro, hence every "for all F" theorem is a schema. The escape is the standard one from the dependently-typed world (Abbott–Altenkirch–Ghani, "Categories of Containers"), and it lands better in your setting than in its native one: reify the functor as data. A container is a pair (S, P) — a shape predicate on wires and a position relation P(s, p) — with extension F⟨S,P⟩[X](w) := ∃s f. w =βη ⟨s, f⟩ ∧ S(s) ∧ ∀p. P(s,p) → X(f p). Since S and P are relations, *the class of containers is second-order quantifiable*, so "every container functor is monotone," "every container has an initial algebra and final coalgebra," "container functors preserve tracked isos" are single theorems with ∀S∀P out front, not schemata. The μν engine's per-instance monotonicity obligations vanish wholesale for this class. What containers don't cover is contravariance — X ↦ (X → 2) is not a container — and that boundary is exactly where the macro discipline resumes; note the pleasant irony that your Berger-tree function space is a *container presentation of the elements of a non-container functor's fixed point*, i.e., the trees smuggle a negative-variance object into wire-land, which is worth understanding as a general technique rather than a one-off.

**Quotient containers, because your quotients are free.** The wedge functor from Freyd's characterization is not a plain container — X ∨ X glues (0, ⊤) to (1, ⊥), a pushout. In type theory this needs quotient containers with attendant machinery (Abbott–Altenkirch–Ghani–McBride); in your system a quotient is just a coarser PER, so a quotient container is a container plus a definable relation on its extension, at zero foundational cost. This is the right general functor class for the library: **container + PER-quotient**, closed under sums, products, composition, and fixed points. The interval construction now factors as: T = the bipointed theory (Tier-A wire), F = the wedge quotient-container lifted to Mod(T), interval = νF via the ∃-encoding, uniqueness via SDin. Every ingredient is an instance of general Tier-A/B machinery.

**Structured final coalgebras via distributive laws — this is where the arithmetic lives.** The interval is not merely a final coalgebra; it is a final coalgebra *carrying algebraic structure* (bipointed, midpoint), and the general seat for that is the bialgebraic one: a distributive law λ : Σ∘B ⇒ B∘Σ between an algebraic signature Σ and a behavior functor B lifts B to Σ-algebras, makes the final B-coalgebra a Σ-algebra canonically, and — the operational payoff — Bartels' λ-coiteration / the Turi–Plotkin GSOS format turns any definition of the form "the head and tail of op(x, y) are given by such-and-such in terms of heads and tails of x, y" into a unique tracked operation on νB that respects bisimilarity, with the respect-proof discharged once per *format*, not once per operation. Rutten's behavioral differential equations for streams are exactly this format, and Niqui's thesis work on formalized exact real arithmetic (coinductive continued-fraction/Möbius arithmetic in Coq, λ-coiteration explicitly) is the obscure-but-directly-on-point source for how real arithmetic falls out. In your system a behavioral differential equation is a Y-term, the format theorem is provable at container generality (so it is one internal theorem), and midpoint, negation, and digit-shifts on the signed-digit ν are its instances. The algebraic counterweight is Escardó–Simpson's universal characterization of the interval as the free iterative midpoint algebra on its endpoints — an *initial-algebra-flavored* characterization — and the honest deep statement of your reals block is that the interval is the object where the μ-side (free midpoint structure) and ν-side (final observation structure) characterizations meet, related by precisely such a law. Proving both characterizations against the same PER, internally, would be the pilot's crowning theorem.

**The instance zoo, to calibrate generality.** Cantor space is ν(2 × X) with the trivial quotient; Baire space is ν(ℕ × X), which under the continued-fraction reading is Pavlović–Pratt's coalgebra of the irrationals, with the Gauss map as the structure morphism; the p-adic integers ℤ_p are ν(p × X) with the carry-arithmetic PER, and become ring objects by distributive laws in exactly the midpoint pattern; the full line and half-line come from the continued-fraction functor. More consequentially: a PER on stream-wires *is* a represented space in the TTE sense, tracked maps are the computable maps, and so the entire represented-spaces/QCB framework of computable analysis (Bauer's realizability formulation) is the native reading of "PERs whose fields consist of ν-carrier wires" — the category of "spaces" you get for free is computable analysis's category, with ℝ the signed-digit instance. Groups: Grp = Mod(Lawvere theory), a Tier-B category; topological/Polish groups are group objects in represented spaces, i.e., the same Tier-B pattern with stream-quotient carriers; free groups exist both as μ-of-words-plus-quotient and as impredicative ∀-encodings. Sheaf categories from last time slot in as Tier B via the elaborator. And Cat itself: since Tier-A categories are wires, the category of small categories is Tier B, quantifiable.

**The "(small?)" question has a canonical answer: Gabriel–Ulmer duality is the small/large interface.** The pattern running through every example is that *finitely presentable objects are wires* — an fp group is a presentation term, an fp ℚ-algebra is a presentation term, an eventually-periodic stream is a term with finite data — while general objects are relations. A Tier-B category of models is, internally, the Ind-completion of its Tier-A fp skeleton: a general model is a formula asserting a filtered wire-diagram and describing its colimit, which is expressible because filtered colimits of PERs are definable. This is Gabriel–Ulmer duality (Lex theories ↔ locally finitely presentable categories) operating as your library's size discipline: **Tier A holds the theory/fp side, Tier B its ind-completion, and the duality is the dictionary.** The ν-side objects are the ones this classification *doesn't* capture — the interval is nowhere near finitely presentable as an algebra — and that is not a defect but the precise sense in which coinductive objects are the dual citizens: they are pro-finitary on the observation side (every point is determined by its finite-prefix observations, which are wire data), so the library's ontology is fp-presentations and their ind-completions on the μ-side, finite-observations and their pro-style ν-completions on the other, with impredicativity supplying both completions in one shot — no ω-chains, no final sequences, the Adámek/Barr iteration machinery simply not needed because the ∃-encoding is single-shot.

**Completeness of Tier B, and why it closes the loop.** Products of a wire-indexed family of PERs are ∀-encodings; equalizers are conjunctions; hence Tier-B categories built from Mod(T) have limits over all Tier-A diagrams — the syntactic shadow of Hyland's small-complete-category phenomenon, now doing concrete work: cofree coalgebras and free algebras come from these limits plus μν, Kan extensions along Tier-A functors are ends, i.e., ∀-encodings with SDin wedge conditions, and the cohesion modalities from the physics layer are these Kan extensions. So the same three primitives — impredicative quantification, SDin, containers — generate the reals, the algebra, and the modal apparatus, which is the "special case of a more general construction" you asked for in its strongest form.
